✔ THE VERDICT: THEY NEVER STOPPED

The archaeological evidence is CLEAR: Indigenous Californian coastal peoples — Chumash, Ohlone, and related communities — were not passive victims of colonization. They were active, strategic agents who selectively adapted, resisted, and preserved their foodways under conditions of extreme colonial pressure.

The "loss narrative" promoted by colonial-era sources is contradicted by zooarchaeology, archaeobotany, isotopic analysis, and ethnohistory. These food traditions were defended, maintained, and transmitted.

Three post-contact villages — Helo’, Humaliwo, and Talepop — document exactly how. The feast was not nostalgia. It was infrastructure. And underneath it all: a hidden transcript the Spanish never cracked.

◆ REFUGE ZONES: CLASSIFIED — THE HINTERLAND ESCAPE NETWORK

Not everyone went to the missions. Some people fled. Some never went. And the zones they retreated to — the hinterlands — hold some of the most powerful evidence in the entire record.

Three shellmound sites on the Marin Peninsula. Eight AMS radiocarbon dates. Occupation extending deep into the mission era. No glass beads. No European ceramics. Not because they were unavailable — because they were refused.

◆ CLASSIFIED DOSSIER
SCHNEIDER, TSIM D. (2015)
"Placing refuge and the archaeology of Indigenous hinterlands in colonial California"
American Antiquity 80(4):695–713
Schneider is a citizen of the Federated Indians of Graton Rancheria — a Coast Miwok and Southern Pomo tribe whose federal recognition was revoked in 1958 and restored in 2000. He is studying his own people's landscape.
▼ BEYOND COLONIAL REACH: A MAP THEY NEVER MADE
▼ THREE SHELLMOUNDS. EIGHT DATES. ALL INCRIMINATING. ▼
AMS radiocarbon dating of previously collected materials from CA-MRN-114, CA-MRN-115, and CA-MRN-328 on the Marin Peninsula establishes mission-era occupation at all three sites. Clamshell disk beads. Obsidian projectile points. Abalone ornaments. Zero glass beads. Zero European ceramics. In a region where those goods were circulating — that absence is a choice. (Schneider 2015:702–705)
1 Schneider's "resistance-memory-refuge" framework moves past the binary of domination and resistance. These shellmounds weren't just hiding spots. They were culturally meaningful landscapes — places that sustained identity, subsistence knowledge, and social memory across generations of colonial disruption. (Schneider 2015:695–696, 708–709)
2 Shellmounds served as places of refuge for Coast Miwok who fled missions, received furloughs (paseos), or avoided missionization entirely. The missions had a boundary. The shellmounds were on the other side of it. (Schneider 2015:697–701)
◆ LOPE INIGO KNEW WHAT HE WAS DOING
◆ THE COLONIAL SYSTEM'S MOST PATIENT WITNESS ◆
Lope Inigo, an Ohlone man baptized at Mission Santa Clara, successfully petitioned the Mexican government for title to lands containing shellmounds — almost certainly holding his ancestors' remains. He requested, before his death in 1864, to be buried in one of them. He spent his life inside the colonial system and still chose, at the end of it, to return to the same ground his community had used for millennia. (Schneider 2015:699)
3 Schneider's concept of "Indigenous hinterlands" reframes colonial California entirely. Not a story of total domination — a story of strategic geography. The missions had walls. The hinterlands had fish. And in 1864 an old man asked to be buried there. (Schneider 2015:695–713)

◆ GONE FISHING (AGAINST ORDERS): THE CURRY DISPATCH

A science journalist interviewed the researchers. Walked the sites. Read the reports. What he wrote cuts right to the human story underneath all the faunal data.

Mission Santa Clara. China Camp State Park. Toms Point trading post. Three sites. One conclusion: the old ways were not gone. They were 100 to 300 miles away, chemically traceable, and still in use.

CURRY, ANDREW (2022)
"California's coastal homelands: How Native Americans defied Spanish missionaries and preserved their way of life"
Archaeology 75(3), May/June 2022
Field reporting + interviews: Panich (Santa Clara U.), Schneider (UC Santa Cruz), archaeobotanist GeorgeAnn DeAntoni (UC Santa Cruz)
► THE OBSIDIAN THAT BROKE THE CASE OPEN
► 100–300 MILES. UNDER MISSIONARY SUPERVISION. ►
Obsidian flakes recovered from mission-era deposits at Mission Santa Clara were chemically traced to the Napa Valley and the Sierra Nevada — nearly one hundred to three hundred miles from the mission. Under the noses of missionaries. While under surveillance. Precontact exchange networks continued without interruption. (Curry 2022)
1 Stone tool manufacturing at mission sites continued into the 1840s. The skills did not atrophy. The technology did not disappear. The knowledge was transmitted across every decade of colonial occupation. (Curry 2022)
2 At Toms Point — a post-contact trading post site — archaeological work by Panich and Schneider documents the full range of material persistence: fish, plant remains, traditional tools, and exchange goods sourced from far outside colonial territory. (Curry 2022)
◆ BOTTLE GLASS. PORCELAIN SHARDS. TRADITIONAL FORMS.
◆ COLONIAL WASTE. INDIGENOUS TECHNOLOGY. ◆
Indigenous people at Mission Santa Clara knapped broken bottle glass and porcelain sherds into traditionally shaped projectile points. Colonial refuse. Transformed into Indigenous technology. In a form their family had always made. Everyday resistance rarely looks like rebellion. Usually it looks like this. (Curry 2022)
3 Curry's article names what the scholarly sources imply: a person knapping bottle glass into an arrowhead isn't just making a tool. They are exercising a form of knowledge their community refused to surrender. The medium changed. The practice didn't. (Curry 2022)

▶ SITE SCRIPT / CAPTIONS (APG 303 submission — Matthew Kyle, April 2026)
INTRO: When Spanish missionaries arrived on the California coast in 1769, they assumed they could replace thousands of years of Indigenous food knowledge with European agriculture. The archaeological record tells a different story — one of strategic resistance, adaptation, and survival. SECTION 1 — PRE-CONTACT FOODWAYS & FEASTING (Gamble 2015): Chumash communities maintained elaborate feasting ceremonies after Spanish colonization as a deliberate strategy to preserve social identity and political authority — what Gamble calls a “hidden transcript” of cultural continuity operating beneath colonial surveillance. Material assemblages from three post-contact villages (Helo’, Humaliwo, Talepop) show steatite cooking vessel production not merely persisting but accelerating under colonialism — vessels grew larger and more elaborately decorated, appearing in greater quantities in historical-period cemeteries (Gamble 2015:115–130). SECTION 2 — MOLECULAR TESTIMONY (Eerkens et al. 2025): Dental calculus proteomics on fifteen Ohlone individuals buried at a mission outpost in Pacifica detected deer, otter, and beaver proteins in virtually every individual — evidence that traditional wild game persisted in the diet at the molecular level, inside bodies the missions could not fully control. Nicotine appeared across all sex and age groups, demonstrating that tobacco use survived mission supervision. Nearly all individuals were buried with traditional grave goods in defiance of Catholic doctrine (Eerkens et al. 2025: ch. 5, 7, 8). SECTION 3 — BOTANICAL EVIDENCE (Reddy 2015): Over 400,000 carbonized seeds from Mission Period sites in the Los Angeles Basin reveal that native wild grasses — Maygrass at 67.9% and Little Barley at 13.4% — dominate plant assemblages across every context analyzed. Introduced European domesticates constitute under 1% of mourning ceremony offerings. A single burned basket offering contained over 267,000 Maygrass seeds. In the most sacred contexts, colonial plants were not just absent — they were actively excluded (Reddy 2015:54–60). SECTION 4 — INDIGENOUS HINTERLANDS (Schneider 2015): Not everyone entered the mission system. Landscape archaeology at hinterland sites — areas geographically removed from Spanish colonial settlements — shows strong continuity with pre-contact subsistence practices, particularly intensive fishing. Fish bone assemblages at these sites match pre-contact patterns in species diversity and processing methods, suggesting that communities who maintained independence from the missions preserved traditional fishing knowledge largely intact. Schneider terms these spaces "Indigenous hinterlands" — zones of refuge and cultural persistence (Schneider 2015:697–710). SECTION 5 — THE HUMAN STORY (Curry 2022): Fish bones recovered from mission-period deposits represent species caught far from mission grounds — suggesting Indigenous people were traveling to traditional fishing sites outside mission boundaries on their own terms. Science journalist Andrew Curry, drawing on interviews with active researchers, frames these ordinary objects — bones, seeds, grinding stones — as the material record of everyday resistance (Curry 2022). CONCLUSION: The loss narrative embedded in colonial-era accounts is contradicted by zooarchaeological, archaeobotanical, isotopic, and ethnohistoric evidence. Indigenous Californian coastal peoples were strategic agents who defended and maintained their foodways — inside missions, outside them, and in the spaces in between. The truth was in the middens all along.

APG 303: Archaeology of the Americas — Annotated Bibliography

Matthew Kyle  |  April 12, 2026

Topic: Indigenous Subsistence Persistence and Agency in Colonial California

The dominant narrative of Spanish colonization in California treats the ever-changing Indigenous culture as something that happened to Native peoples rather than something they actively forged on their own terms. Archaeological research has steadily dismantled that view. Chumash and related coastal communities made materially traceable decisions about which subsistence practices to maintain, intensify, or abandon; choices visible in faunal assemblages, carbonized plant remains, shell middens, feasting vessels, and mission-period waste heaps. I chose this topic because it demands a more honest account of what colonialism actually accomplished, and what it failed to. The sources here trace subsistence decisions before and after European arrival and what they reveal about identity, resilience, and the practical limits of colonial power.

Gamble, Lynn H.
2015  Subsistence practices and feasting rites: Chumash identity after European colonization. Historical Archaeology 49(2):115–135.

  • Author credentials: Gamble is a Professor Emerita of Anthropology at UC Santa Barbara, where she also completed her PhD, and the author of The Chumash World at European Contact (2008, UC Press). Four decades of fieldwork and publication in the Santa Barbara Channel region make her the primary authority on Chumash political economy, exchange systems, and culture contact. The article lists her affiliation as UC Santa Barbara.
  • Methods: Gamble draws on archaeological assemblages from three post-contact Chumash villages: Helo’ (CA-SBA-46), Humaliwo (CA-LAN-264), and Talepop (CA-LAN-229). She analyzes steatite cooking vessels, decorated mortars and pestles, and faunal remains alongside ethnohistorical sources: Spanish mission records and early twentieth-century ethnographic interviews that John P. Harrington conducted with Chumash consultants (pp. 115–118). The three-site comparative structure allows her to document variation in how deeply Spanish influence penetrated different communities.
  • Main points/conclusions: Gamble demonstrates that Chumash communities deliberately sustained traditional subsistence practices and feasting ceremonies as mechanisms for preserving social identity and political authority. Steatite vessel production did not decline under colonialism; it accelerated. Vessels became larger and more elaborately decorated following the introduction of metal tools, and their frequency in historical-period cemeteries increased (pp. 118–120). Traditional foods (acorns, chia, fish) continued to anchor feasting events documented across sites (pp. 123–124). Yet the degree of Spanish penetration varied: Talepop assemblages include cattle bone from nearby ranchos, while Helo’ residents incorporated almost none of the colonizers’ foodstuffs, apparently resisting introduced cultigens as deliberately as they resisted baptism (pp. 125–130). Gamble frames this persistence as a “hidden transcript” of cultural continuity operating beneath colonial surveillance (p. 130).
  • Relation to topic and other sources: This article establishes my foundational argument — colonization did not erase Indigenous food traditions — and anchors the southern California focus of this research. Its analysis of feasting as social and political practice, not merely subsistence behavior, provides the interpretive framework that Reddy (2015) populates with botanical evidence and Eerkens et al. (2025) extends to the level of individual dietary chemistry. Schneider (2015) represents the spatial complement: where Gamble examines persistence within post-contact villages, Schneider examines it in hinterland refuges beyond colonial reach.
  • Interesting detail for public engagement project: Steatite cooking vessels not only survive in post-contact deposits but grow larger and more elaborate, appearing in increasing quantities as burial goods in historical-period cemeteries (pp. 118–120). Rather than atrophying under colonial pressure, a distinctly Chumash cooking technology flourished. The detail inverts the expected narrative, which makes it effective for public engagement — the archaeological record not only refutes the erasure narrative but actively contradicts it.

Eerkens, Jelmer W., Lee M. Panich, Christopher Canzonieri, and Christopher Zimmer (editors)
2025  Native Persistence at a California Mission Outpost: The Bioarchaeology and History of the Asistencia de San Pedro y San Pablo. University of Florida Press, Gainesville.

  • Author credentials: Eerkens directs UC Davis’s Archaeometry Lab, specializing in diet and behavior through skeletal chemistry. Panich (Santa Clara University) researches Indigenous-colonial interactions in California and co-edited Indigenous Landscapes and Spanish Missions (2014) with Schneider, who appears later in this bibliography. Canzonieri brings twenty-five years of California CRM practice; Zimmer represents the Amah Mutsun Tribal Band — the state-designated Most Likely Descendants group for south-San Francisco through Monterey Bay — embedding Indigenous community perspectives in the volume’s structure.
  • Methods: The volume reports on fifteen Ohlone individuals uncovered during utility construction at the Sánchez Adobe in Pacifica, the site of the eighteenth-century Asistencia de San Pedro y San Pablo, an outpost of Mission San Francisco. Methods include paleoanthropological analysis, dental calculus proteomics (LC-MS) to identify food proteins in calcified plaque, stable isotope analysis of teeth and bone, metabolomic analysis (UPLC-MS) targeting nicotine and cacao-related compounds, mortuary analysis of grave goods, and cross-referencing of bioarchaeological profiles against mission sacramental records (pp. 1–3, 20–21).
  • Main points/conclusions: Traditional lifeways persisted at the molecular level. Dental calculus proteomics detected deer, otter, and beaver proteins in virtually every individual, confirming continued reliance on traditionally hunted game even within a mission outpost context (chapter 7). Nicotine appeared across sexes and age groups, demonstrating that tobacco use — a practice of deep spiritual significance in California Indigenous societies — survived mission supervision (chapter 8). Nearly all individuals were buried with grave goods in direct defiance of Catholic doctrine (chapter 5). Some colonial foods (cow’s milk, pomegranate, cacao) appeared more frequently in males and younger individuals, suggesting that age and gender shaped the pace of dietary incorporation rather than colonial policy determining it uniformly. Eight burials were matched by name to mission death records (chapter 11).
  • Relation to topic and other sources: This volume extends the geographic scope beyond southern California, demonstrating that subsistence persistence was not a regional peculiarity of the Chumash but a pattern characterizing Indigenous communities across the California mission corridor. Its methodological distinctiveness is equally significant: where Gamble traces persistence through material culture and Reddy through carbonized seeds, Eerkens et al. recover it from inside individual bodies. This source also strengthens Gamble’s claim that rates of dietary adoption varied by gender and age group.
  • Interesting detail for public engagement project: A teenager named Tarsi was buried with bird bone whistles, obsidian projectile points, quartz crystals, and bat ray barbs — objects that set him apart from every other burial at the site and suggest a form of spiritual or ritual distinction not reducible to political status — and his identity was recovered from 240-year-old mission death records (pp. 99–100, 220–221). A named person, a specific age, particular objects chosen for burial: that kind of particularity is so uncommon it could not go unmentioned in the bibliography.

Reddy, Seetha N.
2015  Feeding family and ancestors: persistence of traditional Native American lifeways during the Mission Period in coastal Southern California. Journal of Anthropological Archaeology 37:48–66.

  • Author credentials: Reddy is an independent paleoethnobotanist at Reddy Anthropology Consulting in Davis, California, specializing in plant remains from California coastal contexts. The article emerged from the Playa Vista Archaeological and Historical Project in the Ballona Lagoon area of Los Angeles, which produced a Mission Period botanical assemblage larger than most single-site studies generate. Her institutional affiliation is listed as Reddy Anthropology Consulting.
  • Methods: Reddy analyzes over 400,000 carbonized seeds from more than 300 liters of floated sediment at two adjacent Mission Period sites: CA-LAN-62/H, containing mortuary deposits and mourning ceremony features, and CA-LAN-211/H, a domestic feasting midden. Together they yield three analytically distinct context types: mortuary offerings, mourning ceremony deposits, and domestic feasting waste (pp. 51–55). This allows for a granular comparison of wild versus introduced plant use across sacred and everyday contexts.
  • Main points/conclusions: Native wild grasses — Maygrass (Phalaris sp., 67.9%) and Little Barley (Hordeum pusillum, 13.4%) — dominate plant materials across all three contexts, constituting 88.7% of mourning ceremony offerings (pp. 54–57). Introduced European domesticates account for under 1% of mourning features and only 1.6% of domestic feasting deposits (pp. 57–60). The pattern reveals a deliberate cultural logic: the more sacred or ritual the context, the lower the adoption of colonial plant foods — suggesting intentional exclusion rather than limited access. Reddy also challenges prior ecological models claiming that Spanish cattle herds had immediately transformed the regional landscape (pp. 60–62).
  • Relation to topic and other sources: This article provides the botanical evidence that Gamble’s material culture framework demands but cannot supply. Reddy broadens the research to the Gabrieliño/Tongva of the Los Angeles Basin — a different people, different geography, yet the same pattern of strategic subsistence persistence. The title’s framing, “feeding family and ancestors,” captures the argument that food choices in ritual contexts were acts of social and spiritual continuity, not merely dietary habit — a claim that resonates directly with the mortuary evidence Eerkens et al. (2025) recover through bioarchaeological chemistry at a northern California outpost site.
  • Interesting detail for public engagement project: Mourning ceremony features at CA-LAN-62 preserved asphaltum-lined baskets containing hundreds of thousands of carbonized seeds — over 267,000 Maygrass seeds from a single burned basket offering alone (Table 3). In the contexts where colonial disruption might be expected to register most forcefully — sacred ceremonies performed in proximity to missions — traditional plant foods were offered in extraordinary quantities. Resistance was practiced in the most intimate human act available: feeding and honoring the dead.

Schneider, Tsim D.
2015  Placing refuge and the archaeology of Indigenous hinterlands in colonial California. American Antiquity 80(4):695–713.

  • Author credentials: Schneider is an Assistant Professor of Anthropology at UC Santa Cruz and a citizen of the Federated Indians of Graton Rancheria — a Coast Miwok and Southern Pomo tribe whose federal recognition was revoked in 1958 and restored in 2000, an experience that directly informs his research agenda. He co-edited Indigenous Landscapes and Spanish Missions (2014, University of Arizona Press) with Panich. His institutional affiliation is listed in the article.
  • Methods: Schneider combines archival research and reanalysis of early twentieth-century excavation reports with AMS radiocarbon dating of previously collected materials from three shellmound sites on the Marin Peninsula: CA-MRN-114, CA-MRN-115, and CA-MRN-328. Eight AMS dates from charred basketry, wood plank fragments, shell, and charcoal establish occupation extending well into the mission period (pp. 702–704, Table 1). He analyzes clamshell disk beads, obsidian projectile points, and abalone ornaments for their chronological significance, and notes the absence of glass beads and European ceramics as evidence of intentional traditional practice rather than mere inaccessibility (pp. 704–705).
  • Main points/conclusions: Schneider develops the concept of “Indigenous hinterlands” — landscapes beyond direct colonial control that Native Californians actively used, revisited, and invested with cultural meaning during and after the mission era. Shellmounds functioned as places of refuge for Coast Miwok who fled missions, received furloughs (paseos), or avoided missionization entirely (pp. 697–701). His “resistance-memory-refuge” framework moves beyond the binary of domination and resistance to show how culturally salient landscapes sustained identity, subsistence knowledge, and social memory across generations of colonial disruption (pp. 695–696, 708–709). The article also serves a disciplinary critique: studying colonialism exclusively through mission sites systematically obscures Indigenous agency.
  • Relation to topic and other sources: This is the only source in this bibliography focused entirely on communities outside the mission system — those who resisted incorporation rather than navigating it from within. Gamble, Reddy, and Eerkens et al. document persistence under surveillance; Schneider documents it beyond surveillance. Together, the four scholarly sources map the full spatial range of Indigenous subsistence agency, from within mission outposts to hinterland refuges beyond colonial reach.
  • Interesting detail for public engagement project: Lope Inigo, an Ohlone man baptized at Mission Santa Clara, successfully petitioned the Mexican government for title to lands containing shellmounds that almost certainly held his ancestors’ remains, and requested before his death in 1864 to be buried in one of them (p. 699). He spent his life inside the colonial system and still chose, at the end of it, to return to the same ground his community had used for millennia. The detail conveys, without abstraction, what the hinterlands meant.

Curry, Andrew
2022  California’s coastal homelands: How Native Americans defied Spanish missionaries and preserved their way of life. Archaeology 75(3). May/June 2022.
archaeology.org/issues/may-june-2022/letters-from/california-s-coastal-homelands/, accessed April 12, 2026.

  • Author credentials: Curry is a freelance science journalist based in Berlin with a BA from Georgetown University and an MA from Stanford University. A contributing editor at Archaeology magazine, he writes regularly for National Geographic, Science, Nature, and Wired, and his work appears in The Best American Science and Nature Writing. He is not an archaeologist; he was chosen partly for this reason, as his article represents how these findings reach a public audience beyond the specialized readership of academic journals.
  • Methods: Curry’s method is field reporting and scholarly synthesis. He conducts original interviews with active researchers including Panich (Santa Clara University), Schneider (UC Santa Cruz), and archaeobotanist GeorgeAnn DeAntoni (UC Santa Cruz). He translates their findings through engaging narrative storytelling rather than original data analysis, presenting peer-reviewed findings for a general readership.
  • Main points/conclusions: Recent excavations at Mission Santa Clara, China Camp State Park shellmounds, and the Toms Point trading post show that Native Californians maintained traditional subsistence practices, trade networks, and material culture well into the colonial period. Mission-era obsidian flakes at Santa Clara were chemically traced to the Napa Valley and the Sierra Nevada — nearly one hundred to three hundred miles from home — demonstrating that precontact exchange networks persisted under missionary supervision. Stone tool manufacturing continued into the 1840s. People also repurposed European waste — bottle glass, porcelain sherds — into traditionally shaped projectile points, transforming colonial material into Indigenous technology.
  • Relation to topic and other sources: As my only popular source, this article is useful in a different way from the scholarly pieces. Where the scholarly sources make the argument through excavation data, Curry makes it through narrative — and the same argument persists. His reporting draws directly on Schneider’s shellmound research and Panich’s mission excavations, both of which also underlie Eerkens et al. (2025), confirming that the academic consensus this bibliography engages is active and publicly accessible.
  • Interesting detail for public engagement project: Panich describes Indigenous people at Mission Santa Clara knapping broken bottle glass and porcelain into projectile points — colonial waste remade into traditional tools. What makes the detail work for a public audience is its ordinariness: people improvising with what was at hand, in a form they already knew. Everyday resistance rarely looks like rebellion; usually it looks like this.

Site Script / Captions

APG 303: Archaeology of the Americas  |  Public Engagement Product  |  Matthew Kyle, April 2026

This project is a single-page HTML/CSS/JavaScript website built as a parody of early-2000s GeoCities “ancient mysteries” and conspiracy theory sites — the kind that would dramatically claim the pyramids were built by aliens or that the government was hiding Bigfoot. The aesthetic is deliberately over-the-top: animated marquee text, dark starfield backgrounds, flashing visitor counters, sensationalist headers, and popup ads styled as Windows 98 dialog boxes. That framing is the vehicle for accurate, cited archaeological content. All factual claims are in my own words and sourced with in-text citations and page numbers. A vocabulary tooltip system built into the site allows non-specialist readers to click underlined technical terms (“neophytes,” “faunal assemblages,” “dental calculus”) for plain-English definitions without interrupting the main text. The site is accessible via any web browser with no special software required.

Topic: Indigenous subsistence persistence and agency in colonial California — specifically, how Native Californian coastal peoples maintained, adapted, and continued their traditional food practices during and after the Spanish mission period (roughly 1769–1833), rather than abandoning them under colonial pressure. The core argument, drawn from five sources, is that the archaeological record shows Native people as active agents making real choices, not passive victims of cultural erasure.

Source Pages (five dedicated tabs)

Each source has its own tab on the site, introduced with a “classified dossier” block displaying the full citation prominently. Together the five pages present five distinct methodological lines of evidence that all converge on the same conclusion:

Gamble (2015) — Faunal assemblages, steatite cooking vessels, and mortars from three post-contact Chumash villages show that traditional subsistence practices and feasting ceremonies continued as deliberate strategies for preserving social identity and political authority. Steatite vessel production did not decline under colonialism; it accelerated, with vessels growing larger and more elaborate in historical-period deposits (Gamble 2015:118–120).

Eerkens et al. (2025) — Dental calculus proteomics (LC-MS) on fifteen Ohlone individuals from a California mission outpost detected deer, otter, and beaver proteins in virtually every individual, confirming continued reliance on traditionally hunted game under mission supervision. Nicotine appeared across all age groups, indicating tobacco use — of deep spiritual significance — survived mission oversight (Eerkens et al. 2025: chapters 7–8).

Reddy (2015) — Analysis of over 400,000 carbonized seeds from Mission Period sites in the Los Angeles Basin shows that native wild grasses (Maygrass 67.9%, Little Barley 13.4%) dominate plant assemblages across all context types, including mourning ceremonies and feasting middens. Introduced European crops constitute under 1% of the most sacred contexts, suggesting deliberate cultural logic rather than limited access (Reddy 2015:54–60).

Schneider (2015) — Landscape archaeology at shellmound sites on the Marin Peninsula, combined with AMS radiocarbon dating, documents occupation extending into the mission period by Coast Miwok communities who fled missions or avoided them entirely. Fish bone assemblages and the deliberate absence of European trade goods at these “Indigenous hinterland” sites demonstrate intentional maintenance of pre-contact subsistence patterns beyond colonial reach (Schneider 2015:697–710).

Curry (2022) — Journalistic synthesis drawing on interviews with active researchers. Obsidian at Mission Santa Clara was chemically traced to the Napa Valley and Sierra Nevada (100–300 miles away), confirming pre-contact exchange networks persisted under missionary supervision. People also knapped European bottle glass and porcelain into traditionally shaped projectile points — colonial waste remade into Indigenous technology (Curry 2022).
