▶ SITE SCRIPT / CAPTIONS (APG 303 submission — Matthew Kyle, April 2026)
INTRO: When Spanish missionaries arrived on the California coast in 1769, they assumed they could replace thousands of years of Indigenous food knowledge with European agriculture. The archaeological record tells a different story — one of strategic resistance, adaptation, and survival. SECTION 1 — PRE-CONTACT FOODWAYS & FEASTING (Gamble 2002): Chumash communities in the Santa Barbara region maintained elaborate feasting ceremonies after Spanish colonization as a deliberate strategy to preserve social identity and political authority. Material evidence — steatite cooking vessels, mortars, faunal assemblages, and refuse deposits — shows traditional food preparation technologies persisting in post-contact deposits. Notably, steatite cooking vessels, a distinctly Chumash technology, continued to appear in colonial-era archaeological contexts, indicating that the Chumash maintained not just what they ate but specifically how they cooked it (Gamble 2002:75–88). SECTION 2 — MISSION LIFE AND FAUNAL RESISTANCE (Arkush 2011): Zooarchaeological analysis of five Spanish mission sites in northern California associated with Ohlone communities reveals wild game and fish species not found in formal mission ration records. Despite the rigid controls of mission life, neophytes continued to hunt, fish, and gather traditional foods on their own initiative — evidence of cultural agency and persistence rather than assimilation (Arkush 2011:64–76). SECTION 3 — BOTANICAL EVIDENCE (Panich, Cuthrell & Schneider 2014): Starch grain residues recovered from grinding stones in mission-period contexts matched native wild plant species — not the European crops the mission system promoted. Macrobotanical and phytolith evidence from coastal Southern California sites confirms that acorns and wild seeds continued to be processed and consumed during the Mission Period, framing food preparation as an act of cultural and spiritual continuity tied to ancestral identity (Panich, Cuthrell & Schneider 2014:898–904). SECTION 4 — INDIGENOUS HINTERLANDS (Schneider 2015): Not everyone entered the mission system. Landscape archaeology at hinterland sites — areas geographically removed from Spanish colonial settlements — shows strong continuity with pre-contact subsistence practices, particularly intensive fishing. Fish bone assemblages at these sites match pre-contact patterns in species diversity and processing methods, suggesting that communities who maintained independence from the missions preserved traditional fishing knowledge largely intact. Schneider terms these spaces "Indigenous hinterlands" — zones of refuge and cultural persistence (Schneider 2015:697–710). SECTION 5 — THE HUMAN STORY (Curry 2022): Fish bones recovered from mission-period deposits represent species caught far from mission grounds — suggesting Indigenous people were traveling to traditional fishing sites outside mission boundaries on their own terms. Science journalist Andrew Curry, drawing on interviews with active researchers, frames these ordinary objects — bones, seeds, grinding stones — as the material record of everyday resistance (Curry 2022). CONCLUSION: The loss narrative embedded in colonial-era accounts is contradicted by zooarchaeological, archaeobotanical, isotopic, and ethnohistoric evidence. Indigenous Californian coastal peoples were strategic agents who defended and maintained their foodways — inside missions, outside them, and in the spaces in between. The truth was in the middens all along.

APG 303: Archaeology of the Americas — Annotated Bibliography

Matthew Kyle  |  April 12, 2026

Topic: Indigenous Subsistence Persistence and Agency in Colonial California

This research project explores how Indigenous coastal communities in California — particularly the Chumash and related groups — adapted, maintained, or transformed their subsistence practices in the wake of Spanish colonization beginning in the late 18th century. Rather than framing colonization as a story of total disruption, recent archaeological research reveals a far more nuanced picture: Native Californians actively made choices about which food traditions to continue, intensify, or modify in response to new pressures on their land and mobility. The physical archaeological record — including faunal assemblages, plant remains, shell middens, food-processing tools like mortars and steatite cooking vessels, and mission-period refuse deposits — allows archaeologists to trace these decisions in material terms. I chose this topic because it allows for a nuanced approach to understanding the impacts of colonization on Indigenous life. It also shows Native people as active, strategic agents as opposed to passive victims of conquest. I will examine how archaeologists use physical remains from both prehistoric and post-contact sites to compare subsistence patterns before and after European arrival, and what those changes reveal about Indigenous resilience, resistance, and identity.

Gamble, Lynn H.
2002  Subsistence practices and feasting rites: Chumash identity after European colonization. Historical Archaeology 36(3):74–93.

  • Author credentials: Lynn H. Gamble is Professor Emerita of Anthropology at the University of California, Santa Barbara, where she also received her PhD. She has studied California's Indigenous peoples since the late 1970s and is one of the foremost authorities on Chumash archaeology, with research spanning subsistence practices, political economy, exchange networks, and culture contact. The article lists her affiliation as UC Santa Barbara.
  • Methods: Gamble analyzes archaeological material from multiple post-contact Chumash sites in the Santa Barbara region. Her primary evidence comes from faunal assemblages, food-processing tools (including steatite cooking vessels and mortars), and refuse deposits dating to the post-contact period. She cross-references these material remains with ethnohistorical documents — Spanish mission records and early colonial accounts — to reconstruct patterns of subsistence and feasting after European arrival (pp. 75–78).
  • Main points/conclusions: Gamble argues that the Chumash were active social agents who deliberately maintained traditional subsistence practices and feasting ceremonies after colonization as a mechanism for preserving social identity and political authority. Feasting, she demonstrates, was not merely about food but served crucial functions in sustaining community leadership and social networks. Even as Spanish missions disrupted daily life, material evidence shows that Chumash leaders continued to organize traditional feasts and that traditional food preparation technologies persisted in post-contact deposits (pp. 80–88).
  • Relation to topic and other sources: This is the foundational article for my research. It establishes the theoretical framework I am working with: colonization did not erase Indigenous food traditions, and Native people were not passive victims. It is focused specifically on the Chumash, making it my anchor source. Compared to Arkush (2011), which examines mission neophytes in northern California, Gamble covers a broader range of post-contact contexts in southern California. It differs from Panich et al. (2014) in emphasizing the social and political dimensions of feasting rather than the botanical record.
  • Interesting detail for public engagement project: Steatite (soapstone) cooking vessels — a distinctly Chumash technology — continue to appear in post-contact archaeological deposits, indicating that the Chumash maintained not just what they ate but specifically how they cooked it (p. 82). This tangible detail could help a general audience connect with the idea of food as a quiet, daily form of cultural resistance.

Arkush, Brooke S.
2011  Native responses to European intrusion: cultural persistence and agency among mission neophytes in Spanish colonial northern California. Historical Archaeology 45(4):62–90.

  • Author credentials: Brooke S. Arkush is a Professor of Anthropology at Weber State University, where he has taught since 1990, having received his PhD from the University of California, Riverside. His research covers the prehistory and colonial history of western North America, with particular expertise in Native cultural continuity and change in postcontact contexts. The article lists his affiliation as Weber State University.
  • Methods: Arkush synthesizes archaeological and ethnohistorical data from five Spanish missions in northern California associated with Ohlone (Costanoan) populations. He examines faunal assemblages and material culture from mission-period deposits alongside historical mission records — including baptism registers, ration logs, and administrative correspondence — to evaluate how far neophytes maintained traditional subsistence practices while living under mission supervision (pp. 64–68).
  • Main points/conclusions: Despite the rigid controls of mission life, Ohlone neophytes continued to fish, gather shellfish, hunt game, and collect wild plant foods, supplementing formal mission rations with traditionally obtained foods. Arkush frames this as evidence of cultural agency and persistence rather than assimilation, concluding that the material record shows Indigenous people actively navigated colonial constraints rather than submitting to them (pp. 72–85).
  • Relation to topic and other sources: This article broadens my topic geographically. While Gamble (2002) and Panich et al. (2014) focus on southern California Chumash communities, Arkush examines Ohlone groups in the northern part of the state, providing comparative evidence that subsistence persistence was a widespread pattern of Indigenous response across California, not a single-group phenomenon. It is also the only source among my scholarly articles that focuses exclusively on people living inside the mission system, distinguishing it from Schneider (2015), who examines those who avoided it altogether.
  • Interesting detail for public engagement project: Faunal remains from mission-period deposits include wild game and fish species not found in the missions' formal ration records, meaning neophytes were supplementing their diet with traditional foods entirely on their own initiative (pp. 74–76). This is compelling for public engagement because it shows resistance happening quietly, through everyday acts of eating, rather than through open confrontation.

  • Methods: The article's methodological core is paleoethnobotany — the analysis of plant remains from archaeological contexts. It also introduces the concept of ancestral obligation — the idea that food traditions carry cultural and spiritual weight — which deepens the theoretical framework established by Gamble (2002) and extends Arkush's (2011) emphasis on everyday agency into the realm of meaning and identity.

Schneider, Tsim D.
2015  Placing refuge and the archaeology of Indigenous hinterlands in colonial California. American Antiquity 80(4):695–713.

  • Author credentials: Tsim D. Schneider is an Assistant Professor of Anthropology at the University of California, Santa Cruz and a citizen of the Federated Indians of Graton Rancheria. His research centers on the archaeology of Indigenous persistence, refuge, and hinterland spaces in colonial California. He has published widely on Indigenous-colonial interactions and is co-editor of multiple volumes on the archaeology of Indigenous presence in the Americas. His institutional affiliation is listed in the article.
  • Methods: Schneider uses landscape archaeology, combining regional survey with targeted excavation of sites in hinterland zones — areas at a geographic remove from Spanish missions and colonial settlements. His analysis focuses on faunal assemblages, including fish bone, as well as lithic tools and other material culture recovered from these peripheral sites. He situates individual site assemblages within a broader regional landscape to identify spatial patterns in Indigenous land use during the colonial period (pp. 697–702).
  • Main points/conclusions: Schneider develops the concept of "Indigenous hinterlands" — spaces beyond direct colonial control where Native Californians maintained or re-established traditional lifeways. He argues these areas served as refuges for people who had fled or avoided the mission system, and that the archaeological record at hinterland sites shows strong continuity with pre-contact subsistence practices, particularly intensive fishing. The article challenges the tendency in colonial archaeology to study Indigenous life exclusively through mission sites (pp. 703–710).
  • Relation to topic and other sources: This is the only article among my scholarly sources that focuses on communities living outside and away from the mission system entirely. Arkush (2011) and Panich et al. (2014) examine subsistence within or near missions; Schneider asks what happened for those who resisted incorporation. This distinction is crucial for my topic because it shows that subsistence persistence took multiple forms depending on a community's relationship to colonial power. Together with the other three scholarly articles, a complete picture emerges: persistence inside missions (Arkush; Panich et al.), at the community level through feasting (Gamble), and in spaces of active refuge (Schneider).
  • Interesting detail for public engagement project: Fish bone assemblages at hinterland sites closely match pre-contact fishing patterns in species diversity and processing methods, suggesting that communities who maintained independence from the mission system preserved traditional fishing knowledge and technologies largely intact (pp. 706–707). For public engagement, this detail conveys that resistance to colonialism was practiced at the riverbank and shoreline — in the most ordinary, essential work of feeding a family.

Site Script / Captions

APG 303: Archaeology of the Americas  |  Public Engagement Product  |  Matthew Kyle, April 2026

This website is a parody of early 2000s Geocities-era "conspiracy theory" and "ancient mysteries" websites — the kind of site that would dramatically reveal that the pyramids were built by aliens or that the government was hiding the truth about Bigfoot. The format is deliberately over-the-top: flashing text, dark backgrounds, sensationalist headers, fake "classified dossiers," and pop-up ads styled after Windows 98 dialog boxes. The joke is that the actual content — the archaeology — is genuinely interesting on its own terms, and doesn't need any of the embellishment. The pseudo-scientific presentation is the vehicle; the real academic argument is the cargo.

The topic is Indigenous subsistence persistence and agency in colonial California: specifically, the ways in which Native Californian coastal peoples maintained, adapted, and continued their traditional food practices during and after the Spanish mission period (roughly 1769–1833), rather than simply abandoning them under colonial pressure. The core argument, drawn from five peer-reviewed and journalistic sources, is that the archaeological record — bones, plant remains, cooking tools, site distributions — shows Native people as active agents making real choices, not passive victims of total cultural erasure.

All in-text citations throughout the site follow the format (Author Year:page) in keeping with archaeological citation conventions, e.g. (Gamble 2002:80–88). No information is presented as a direct quote; all content is paraphrased and synthesized in my own words.

HOME PAGE

The home page introduces the site's aesthetic and central claim. The animated header, visitor counter, flashing marquee text, and popup ads establish the parody framing immediately. The marquee text delivers the core thesis in sensationalist form: that the archaeological record contradicts the colonial narrative of cultural erasure. The "THE EVIDENCE IS EVERYWHERE" framing mimics conspiracy rhetoric, but the claim it makes is factually accurate and sourced. The popup ads are written in the same over-the-top style and reference specific findings from the sources (e.g., the Curry 2022 fish bone evidence, the Schneider 2015 hinterlands concept).

THE EVIDENCE (overview page)

This page functions as the site's primary content overview, equivalent to an introduction or abstract. It presents all five sources in condensed form with citations and explains the key methods archaeologists use to study this topic: zooarchaeology (animal bone analysis), paleoethnobotany (plant remains), landscape archaeology, and cross-referencing with historical documents like mission ration logs. The page explains what we know (traditional foodways persisted) and how we know it (material evidence contradicts the written colonial record). A vocabulary tooltip system ("hint text") is built into the page — clicking underlined terms like "neophytes," "faunal assemblages," "phytoliths," or "ration records" opens a plain-English explanation. This system is designed to make technical terminology accessible to a non-specialist reader without interrupting the flow of the main text.

FEASTS OF DEFIANCE — Gamble (2002)

This page is dedicated to Gamble's 2002 article on Chumash feasting and subsistence after colonization. The "classified dossier" block at the top gives the full citation and author name in a styled box, making the source attribution prominent and easy to find. The content covers Gamble's main argument: that Chumash leaders deliberately maintained traditional feasting ceremonies as a political and social strategy, not just a cultural habit. The steatite (soapstone) cooking vessel evidence is highlighted because it is unusually concrete — these are physical objects with a distinct cultural signature that show up in post-contact deposits, meaning the Chumash weren't just eating traditional foods but cooking them in the traditional way (Gamble 2002:82). The sensationalist framing ("THE SOAPSTONE SMOKING GUN") is the parody element; the underlying claim is accurately cited and substantively correct.

THE BONE DOSSIER — Arkush (2011)

This page covers Arkush's 2011 study of five northern California mission sites associated with Ohlone communities. The focus here is on zooarchaeological evidence — specifically, the presence of wild game and fish species in mission-period deposits that do not appear anywhere in the missions' formal ration records (Arkush 2011:74–76). This gap between the written colonial record and the physical archaeological record is the central argument. The page frames this as "eating off the books," which captures the real point: neophytes were supplementing their officially-documented diet with traditionally-obtained food entirely on their own initiative. Arkush's synthesis of material and documentary evidence (baptism registers, ration logs, faunal assemblages) is noted as the method. The page also introduces the concept of cultural agency — the idea that this behavior represents active decision-making rather than passive survival (Arkush 2011:72–85).

SEEDS OF RESISTANCE — Panich, Cuthrell & Schneider (2014)

This page covers the Panich et al. 2014 article, which is distinctive among the sources for its use of paleoethnobotany — plant remains — rather than animal bone evidence. The key finding is that starch grain residues on grinding stones from mission-period contexts matched native wild plant species, not the European crops the missions promoted (Panich, Cuthrell & Schneider 2014:902). This is a microscopic form of evidence, which the page acknowledges directly ("you need a microscope"). Three independent lines of evidence are presented — starch grains, phytoliths, and charred macrobotanical remains — all converging on the same conclusion. The page also addresses the article's broader cultural argument: that continuing to prepare ancestral foods was an act of spiritual and cultural continuity tied to feeding "family and ancestors," not just caloric survival (pp. 901–904). The parody format (dramatic headers, "ACORNS WIN") is the delivery vehicle; the argument is the authors'.

REFUGE ZONES: CLASSIFIED — Schneider (2015)

This page covers Schneider's 2015 article on Indigenous hinterlands — areas geographically removed from Spanish mission settlements where some Native Californians lived outside the colonial system entirely. Schneider's landscape archaeology approach (regional survey combined with targeted excavation) is explained as the method (pp. 697–702). The central finding is that fish bone assemblages at hinterland sites closely match pre-contact fishing patterns — meaning communities that avoided the missions preserved their subsistence knowledge largely intact (pp. 706–707). The page introduces Schneider's coined term "Indigenous hinterlands" and explains its significance: it reframes colonial California as a leaky system rather than a total one. Some people stayed inside the missions; some fled or never went. Both forms of resistance left material traces. This page contributes the geographic and spatial dimension to the site's overall argument.

GONE FISHING!! — Curry (2022)

This page covers the Curry 2022 article from Archaeology magazine, the site's only popular/journalistic source. Curry's role is different from the scholarly authors — he synthesizes peer-reviewed findings for a general audience and provides the human narrative layer that anchors the abstract archaeological evidence in relatable human behavior. The key detail he highlights — fish bones from mission-period deposits representing species from ancestral fishing locations far from the missions — is the same material evidence present in the scholarly sources, but Curry's framing makes explicit what it means at the human level: someone left, traveled to a place their family had always fished, caught fish the way their family had always caught fish, and came back (Curry 2022). The page frames this as "the record of everyday resistance" — quiet, persistent, daily defiance rather than dramatic confrontation. The "GONE FISHING (AGAINST ORDERS)" title is the parody; the argument is substantively accurate and sourced.
