✔ THE VERDICT: THEY NEVER STOPPED

The archaeological evidence is CLEAR: Indigenous Californian coastal peoples — Chumash, Ohlone, and related communities — were not passive victims of colonization. They were active, strategic agents who selectively adapted, resisted, and preserved their foodways under conditions of extreme colonial pressure.

Whether through feasting ceremonies that reinforced political authority, quietly hunting game that never appeared on mission ration logs, grinding acorns on stones in mission compounds, or fishing at ancestral sites far from colonial oversight — the record is consistent across every line of evidence.

The "loss narrative" promoted by colonial-era sources is contradicted by zooarchaeology, archaeobotany, isotopic analysis, and ethnohistory. These food traditions were defended, maintained, and transmitted.

⚠ EVIDENCE FILE #1: FEASTING AS RESISTANCE (THE CHUMASH)
★ SHOCKING FACT ★
Chumash leaders didn't just keep cooking their traditional foods after colonization — they kept hosting ELABORATE FEASTS as a deliberate political strategy to maintain social authority. The Spanish had no idea this was happening. Or maybe they did and couldn't stop it.
1 Faunal assemblages, steatite cooking vessels, mortars, and refuse deposits from post-contact Chumash sites in the Santa Barbara region show that traditional subsistence practices and feasting rites continued after European arrival. Gamble cross-references this material evidence with Spanish mission records to establish the pattern. (Gamble 2002:75–78)
2 Chumash leaders deliberately maintained traditional feasting ceremonies as a mechanism for preserving social identity and political authority — feasting was not just about food but served crucial functions in sustaining community leadership and social networks. (Gamble 2002:80–88)

◆ EVIDENCE FILE #2: MISSION LIFE — THE BONES DON'T LIE

Here is what THEY don't teach you in school. Even inside the mission system — under direct colonial surveillance — Native Californians kept hunting, fishing, and gathering their own food.

⚡ ZOOARCHAEOLOGY BLOWS THE LID OFF ⚡
Faunal assemblages from five Spanish mission sites in northern California — associated with Ohlone (Costanoan) populations — show wild game and fish species that do NOT APPEAR in the missions' formal ration records. Neophytes were supplementing their diet with traditional foods entirely on their own initiative. (Arkush 2011:74–76)
3 Despite the rigid controls of mission life, Ohlone neophytes continued to fish, gather shellfish, hunt game, and collect wild plant foods alongside formal mission rations. Arkush synthesizes faunal data with baptism registers, ration logs, and administrative correspondence to establish this pattern. (Arkush 2011:64–68)

✧ EVIDENCE FILE #3: THE GRINDING STONE REVELATION

CLASSIFIED PALEOETHNOBOTANICAL DATA: Starch grain analysis on grinding stones from mission-period contexts has revealed a truth the colonial record tried to bury.

♦ WHAT THE SEEDS REVEAL ♦
Starch grain residues on grinding stones in mission-period deposits matched native wild plant species — not the European crops the mission system promoted. People were still preparing traditional foods using traditional tools under mission supervision. (Panich, Cuthrell & Schneider 2014:902)
5 Macrobotanical remains (charred seeds and plant fragments) and microbotanical evidence (phytoliths and starch grains) from mission-period sites in coastal Southern California demonstrate that wild seeds, acorns, and other gathered species continued to be processed and consumed during the Mission Period. (Panich, Cuthrell & Schneider 2014:898–901)

NOT EVERYONE WENT TO THE MISSIONS. Some people fled. Some never went. And the archaeological record of what happened in those peripheral zones — the hinterlands — is one of the most powerful datasets we have.

▼ INDIGENOUS HINTERLANDS: CLASSIFIED ▼
Landscape archaeology combining regional survey with targeted excavation of hinterland sites — areas geographically removed from Spanish missions — reveals strong continuity with pre-contact subsistence practices, particularly intensive fishing. ◆ STEATITE SECRETS: THE SOAPSTONE THAT SURVIVED COLONIZATION

Steatite — soapstone — is a soft, heat-resistant stone that the Chumash mastered for cooking. Steatite cooking vessels are a distinctly Chumash technology. When the Spanish arrived, you might expect these vessels to disappear from the archaeological record.

THEY DID NOT DISAPPEAR.

Their continued presence in post-contact deposits is one of the most direct pieces of material evidence for technological and cultural continuity in the entire record.

▶ SITE SCRIPT / CAPTIONS (APG 303 submission — Matthew Kyle, April 2026)
INTRO: When Spanish missionaries arrived on the California coast in 1769, they assumed they could replace thousands of years of Indigenous food knowledge with European agriculture. The archaeological record tells a different story — one of strategic resistance, adaptation, and survival. SECTION 1 — PRE-CONTACT FOODWAYS & FEASTING (Gamble 2002): Chumash communities in the Santa Barbara region maintained elaborate feasting ceremonies after Spanish colonization as a deliberate strategy to preserve social identity and political authority. Material evidence — steatite cooking vessels, mortars, faunal assemblages, and refuse deposits — shows traditional food preparation technologies persisting in post-contact deposits. Notably, steatite cooking vessels, a distinctly Chumash technology, continued to appear in colonial-era archaeological contexts, indicating that the Chumash maintained not just what they ate but specifically how they cooked it (Gamble 2002:75–88). SECTION 2 — MISSION LIFE AND FAUNAL RESISTANCE (Arkush 2011): Zooarchaeological analysis of five Spanish mission sites in northern California associated with Ohlone communities reveals wild game and fish species not found in formal mission ration records. Despite the rigid controls of mission life, neophytes continued to hunt, fish, and gather traditional foods on their own initiative — evidence of cultural agency and persistence rather than assimilation (Arkush 2011:64–76). SECTION 3 — BOTANICAL EVIDENCE (Panich, Cuthrell & Schneider 2014): Starch grain residues recovered from grinding stones in mission-period contexts matched native wild plant species — not the European crops the mission system promoted. Macrobotanical and phytolith evidence from coastal Southern California sites confirms that acorns and wild seeds continued to be processed and consumed during the Mission Period, framing food preparation as an act of cultural and spiritual continuity tied to ancestral identity (Panich, Cuthrell & Schneider 2014:898–904). SECTION 4 — INDIGENOUS HINTERLANDS (Schneider 2015): Not everyone entered the mission system. Landscape archaeology at hinterland sites — areas geographically removed from Spanish colonial settlements — shows strong continuity with pre-contact subsistence practices, particularly intensive fishing. Fish bone assemblages at these sites match pre-contact patterns in species diversity and processing methods, suggesting that communities who maintained independence from the missions preserved traditional fishing knowledge largely intact. Schneider terms these spaces "Indigenous hinterlands" — zones of refuge and cultural persistence (Schneider 2015:697–710). SECTION 5 — THE HUMAN STORY (Curry 2022): Fish bones recovered from mission-period deposits represent species caught far from mission grounds — suggesting Indigenous people were traveling to traditional fishing sites outside mission boundaries on their own terms. Science journalist Andrew Curry, drawing on interviews with active researchers, frames these ordinary objects — bones, seeds, grinding stones — as the material record of everyday resistance (Curry 2022). CONCLUSION: The loss narrative embedded in colonial-era accounts is contradicted by zooarchaeological, archaeobotanical, isotopic, and ethnohistoric evidence. Indigenous Californian coastal peoples were strategic agents who defended and maintained their foodways — inside missions, outside them, and in the spaces in between. The truth was in the middens all along.
